# Slide Layout Patterns

These patterns give Codex a small design vocabulary for no-template decks. Pick two or three and repeat them with discipline.

## Title And Thesis

- Use for opening slides, section starts, or a major claim.
- Compose with a short title, one supporting sentence, and one restrained visual anchor.
- Avoid multiple cards or a busy collage on the first slide.

## Executive Summary Strip

- Use when leaders need fast scanning.
- Arrange three to five findings in a horizontal or vertical strip with consistent labels and one measure per finding.
- Keep the evidence in compact subtext; move detail to appendix slides.

## Evidence With Takeaway

- Use for charts, screenshots, study results, or benchmark data.
- Title states the takeaway. Visual evidence occupies most of the slide. One annotation explains the implication.
- Do not repeat every chart label in body text.

## Comparison Matrix

- Use for options, vendors, scenarios, roadmap choices, or tradeoffs.
- Keep columns consistent and rows short. Use one accent for the preferred or riskiest option.
- If cells become paragraphs, split into multiple slides.

## Process Or Journey

- Use for workflows, patient/customer journeys, implementation plans, and operating models.
- Show three to seven steps with directional flow. Add one risk or decision point only when it changes the story.
- Keep connectors simple; avoid decorative arrows that compete with text.

## Dashboard Or Scorecard

- Use for operating reviews and status updates.
- Combine a few metrics, trend indicators, and status notes. Maintain consistent number formatting.
- Place key variance or action next to the metric, not in a separate paragraph.

## Deep Dive

- Use after a summary slide when a single issue needs detail.
- Use a clear left-to-right or top-to-bottom reading path: context, evidence, implication, action.
- Prefer one strong diagram or table over several small unrelated objects.

## Appendix Detail

- Use for backup evidence that must be retained but should not slow the main narrative.
- Dense content is acceptable only when the slide is explicitly backup material.
- Label appendix slides clearly and keep navigation predictable.
